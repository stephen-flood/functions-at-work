    Chapter 6 Logarithms and Solving for Time

    In the previous section, we used exponential functions to compute the value of compound interest as a function of time. For example, Investing $100 and 25% interest compounded yearly follows the function
    \begin{equation*} F = 100\cdot (1.25)^t \end{equation*}
    Often, you will be given the principal, rate, and frequency of compounding, and you will need to find the amount of time you will have to wait to achieve a certain future balance. If, in the example above, you want to find the time needed to achieve a balance of $4000, you will need to solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} 4000 = 100\cdot (1.25)^t\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is equivalent to
    \begin{equation*} 40 = \cdot (1.25)^t\text{,} \end{equation*}
    but now we are stuck, since the \(t\) is trapped inside the exponent.
    To get further, we will need to "undo" our compound interest calculation. Mathematically, this means that we will need an inverse function.

    Section 6.1 Inverse Functions

    Recall that a one-to-one function sends each input to a different output.

    Example 6.1. Exploring the definition of one-to-one.

    This definition is easiest to apply when you are thinking about functions defined using arrow diagrams.
    If your function is one-to-one, then flipping the arrows gives you a new function that undoes your original function. That new function is called the inverse of the original function.

    Definition 6.2.

    Let \(f\) be a one-to-one function. Its inverse is the function, written \(f^{-1}\text{,}\) which satisfies
    \begin{gather*} f(x) = y \\ \iff \\ x = f^{-1}(y) \end{gather*}
    In ExampleÂ 6.1, the function \(f\) has an inverse function \(f^{-1}\text{,}\) defined by flipping each of the arrows. For example, because \(f(2) = c\text{,}\) we have \(f^{-1}(c) = 2\text{.}\)
    Danger Zone: In general, the inverse \(f^{-1}\) is not the same thing as the reciprocal. In other words, \(f^{-1}(x)\) is not \(\dfrac{1}{f(x)}\) in general.

    Key Idea.

    Inverse functions swich the roles of \(x\) and \(y\) (of input and ouput).

    Example 6.3. Understanding Inverses Graphically.

    Graphically, reversing the roles of input and ouput is the same thing as reflecting the graph of the function across the diagonal line \(y=x\text{.}\)
    Recall that the function \(f^{-1}\) undoes \(f(x)\text{.}\) Mathematically, this means
    \begin{equation*} f\Big( f^{-1}(x) \Big) = x \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} f^{-1}\Big( f(x) \Big) = x \end{equation*}
    Finding inverses and solving equations are two sides of the same coin.

    Exploration 6.1.

    Let \(f(x) = 2x+1\)
    (a)
    Find an equation for \(f^{-1}(x)\)
    Solution.
    First, write down the equation \(y=f(x)\text{.}\) In this case,
    \begin{equation*} y = 2x + 1 \end{equation*}
    Next, solve this equation for \(x\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \amp y \amp \amp= \amp 2x \amp + 1 \\ \amp \amp -1 \amp \amp \amp -1 \\ \amp y \amp -1 \amp= \amp 2x \amp \\ \amp \amp \amp \amp \amp \\ \amp (y \amp -1)\amp= \amp (2x) \amp \\ \times\frac{1}{2} \amp \amp \amp \amp \amp \times\frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2}\amp (y \amp -1)\amp= \amp x \amp \end{align*}
    Finally, reverse the roles of \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) This gives the equation
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{1}{2} ( x - 1) = 0.5 x -0.5 \end{equation*}
    The equation for our inverse is
    \begin{equation*} f^{-1}(x) = 0.5 x -0.5 \end{equation*}
    (b)
    Use your equation for \(f^{-1}\) to evaluate \(f\Big(f^{-1}(x)\Big)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We are given that \(\color{blue} f({\color{red} x}) = 2{\color{red} x} + 1\text{.}\) We have also found that \(f^{-1}(x) = 0.5x - 0.5\text{.}\)
    To compute \({ \color{blue} f\Big({\color{red} f^{-1}(x)}\Big) }\text{,}\) replace every \(\color{red} x\) in the equation for \(\color{blue} f({\color{red} x}) \) with the equation \(\color{red} f^{-1}(x) = 0.5x - 0.5\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} { \color{blue} f\Big({\color{red} f^{-1}(x)}\Big) }= \amp {\color{blue} f\Big( { \color{red} 0.5x - 0.5}\Big)}\\ = \amp {\color{blue} 2\cdot \Big({ \color{red} 0.5x - 0.5}\Big) + 1}\\ = \amp 2\cdot 0.5x - 2\cdot 0.5 + 1\\ = \amp 2\cdot 0.5x - 2\cdot 0.5 + 1\\ = \amp x - 1 + 1\\ = \amp x \end{align*}
    We have shown in this case that \(f\Big(f^{-1}(x)\Big)=x\text{.}\) In other words, we have shown that the function and inverse undo eachother.

    Section 6.2 Logarithms Undo Exponentials

    Our main goal is to use logarithms to solve for time in a compound interest equation. Those equations involve many parts, so we will start with a few slightly simpler questions, in the form of a riddle.

    Exploration 6.2.

    (a)
    How many times do you need to double the number \(10\) to achieve an eight-fold increase?
    Solution.
    Just start doubling 10, and see what works!
    \begin{gather*} 10 \cdot 2 = 20 \\ 10 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 40 \\ 10 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 80 \end{gather*}
    You need to double 10 three times to get from 10 to 80 (an eight-fold increase). In other words,
    \begin{equation*} 80 = 10 \cdot 2^3 \end{equation*}
    (b)
    How many times do you need to double any number \(P\) to achieve an eight fold increase of \(P\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    Our strategy for the first example works the same here:
    \begin{gather*} P \cdot 2 = 2 P \\ P \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 4 P \\ P \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 8 P \end{gather*}
    You need to double any number three times to get an eight-fold increase. This is the same as saying that ,
    \begin{equation*} 8 = 2^3 \end{equation*}
    (c)
    How many times do you need to triple \(P\) to get an 81-fold increase?
    Solution.
    Our strategy for the first example works the same here:
    \begin{gather*} P \cdot 3 = 3 P \\ P \cdot 3 \cdot 3 = 9 P \\ P \cdot 3\cdot 3 \cdot 3 = 21 P\\ P \cdot 3\cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 = 81 P \end{gather*}
    You need to triple any number four times to get an eighty one-fold increase. This is the same as saying that ,
    \begin{equation*} 81 = 3^4 \end{equation*}
    Logarithms are functions that answer the riddle "how many times do I need to multiply by a number \(b\)" to increase by a given factor. More precisely, we make the following definition.

    Definition 6.4.

    Given any numbers \(b,x>0\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \log_{\color{blue}b}(x) = {\color{red} r} \end{equation*}
    is equal to the exponent \(r\) such that
    \begin{equation*} {\color{blue}b}^{\color{red} r} = x \end{equation*}
    We can now rephrase ExplorationÂ 6.2 in logarithmic language. Because \(8 = {\color{blue}2}^{\color{red}3}\text{,}\) we say that \(\log_{\color{blue} 2}(8) = {\color{red}3}\text{.}\) Similarly, because \(81 = {\color{blue}3}^{\color{red}4}\text{,}\) we say that \(\log_{\color{blue} 3} (81) = {\color{red}4}\text{.}\) More precisely, we can now answer the following question.

    Exploration 6.3.

    (a)
    Compute \(\log_2(8)\)
    Solution.
    Written in exponential form, finding \(\log_2(8) = r\) is the same as finding the exponent \(r\) such that
    \begin{equation*} 2^r = 8 \end{equation*}
    Here, because \(8 = {\color{blue}2}^{\color{red}3}\text{,}\) we say that \(\log_{\color{blue} 2}(8) = {\color{red}3}\)
    (b)
    Compute \(\log_3(81)\)
    Solution.
    Written in exponential form, finding \(\log_3(31) = r\) is the same as finding the exponent \(r\) such that
    \begin{equation*} 3^r = 81 \end{equation*}
    Here, because \(81 = {\color{blue}3}^{\color{red}4}\text{,}\) we say that \(\log_{\color{blue} 3} (81) = {\color{red}4}\text{.}\)
    (c)
    Compute \(\log_{10}(1,000,000,000)\)
    Solution.
    Written in exponential form, finding \(\log_10(1,000,000,000) = r\) is the same as finding the exponent \(r\) such that
    \begin{equation*} 10^r = 1,000,000,000 \end{equation*}
    To get the right hand side, we must multiply 10 nine times. That means \(1,000,000,000 = {\color{blue}10}^{\color{red}9}\text{.}\) Therefore, we have that \(\log_{\color{blue} 10} (1,000,000,000) = {\color{red}9}\text{.}\)

    Proposition 6.5.

    Fix any base \(b>1\text{.}\) The function \(y = \log_b(x)\) is the inverse of the the base \(b\) exponential function \(exp_b(x) = b^x\text{.}\)

    The Natural Logarithm for Calculus.

    Recall that the function \(y = e^x\) is called the natural exponential function. We call itâs inverse the natural logarithm.
    Definition 6.6.
    We write \(\ln(x) = \log_e(x)\) where \(e\approx 2.718\dots\)
    In other words, the natural logarithm \(y = \ln(x)\) is the inverse of the natural exponential function \(exp(x) = e^x\text{.}\)
    Exploration 6.4.
    (a)
    Compute \(\ln(\sqrt{e})\)
    Solution.
    There are two ways to answer this question. Algebraically, we know that \(\ln\) undoes base \(e\) exponentiation. So simply rewrite the argument of the function as a power of \(e\)
    \begin{equation*} \ln(\sqrt{e}) = \ln(e^{1/2}) = \frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}
    Alternatively, you can answer the question by playing the riddle game: what does the exponent \(r\) of \(e\) need to be for \(e^{r}\) to equal \(\sqrt{e}\text{?}\)
    We know that \(\sqrt{e} = e^{1/2}\text{.}\) In other words, we need the exponent of \(e\) to be \(\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) That tells us that \(\ln(\sqrt{e}) = \frac{1}{2}\)
    (b)
    Compute \(\ln\left(\dfrac{1}{e}\right)\)
    Solution.
    Again, there are two ways to answer this question. Algebraically, we know that \(\ln\) undoes base \(e\) exponentiation. So simply rewrite the argument of the function as a power of \(e\)
    \begin{equation*} \ln\left(\dfrac{1}{e}\right) = \ln(e^{-1}) = -1 \end{equation*}
    Alternatively, you can answer the question by playing the riddle game: what does the exponent \(r\) of \(e\) need to be for \(e^{r}\) to equal \(\frac{1}{e}\text{?}\)
    We know that \(\frac{1}{e} = e^{-1}\text{.}\) In other words, we need the exponent of \(e\) to be \(-1\text{.}\) That tells us that \(\ln\left(\dfrac{1}{e}\right) = -1\)
    (c)
    Let \(f(x) = \ln(x)\) and \(g(x) = e^x\text{.}\) Simplify \(f(g(3))\text{,}\) \(f(g(-2))\text{,}\) \(f(g(45))\text{,}\) and \(f(g(x))\)
    Solution.
    This is just a more complex way of asking us to simplify the natural log of e to various numbers. Reasoning as above gives
    \begin{equation*} f(g(3)) = \ln(e^3) = 3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(g(-2)) = \ln(e^{-2}) = -2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(g(45)) = \ln(e^{45}) = 45 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(g(x)) = \ln(e^{x}) = x \end{equation*}
    Key Facts.
    The functions \(f(x) = \ln(x)\) and \(g(x) = exp(x) = e^x\) undo eachother.
    \begin{gather*} \ln\left(e^x\right) = x \\ e^{\ln(x)} = x \end{gather*}

    Checkpoint 6.7.

    Simplify \(e^{2\cdot \ln(5)}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} e^{2\cdot \ln(5)} = \amp e^{\ln(5)\cdot 2} \amp \text{ Because } a^{rs} = a^{sr} \\ = \amp \Big(e^{\ln(5)}\Big)^2 \amp \text{ Because } a^{rs} = (a^r)^{s} \\ = \amp \Big(5\Big)^2 \amp \text{ Because } e^{\ln(5)} = 5 \\ = \amp 25 \amp \end{align*}

    Section 6.3 Laws of Logarithms

    Logarithms have three important properties, which will play a central role in our computations.

    Laws of Logarithms.

    \begin{equation*} \ln\Big( x^r \Big)= r\cdot \ln(x) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \ln\Big( x\cdot y \Big)= \ln(x) + \ln(y) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \ln\left( \dfrac{x}{y} \right)= \ln(x) - \ln(y) \end{equation*}

    Exploration 6.5.

    Expand the following completely using the laws of logarithms.
    (a)
    \(\ln(5^3)\)
    Solution.
    When you look at this expression, you see a power, so write down the first law of logarithms \(\ln\Big( x^r \Big)= r\cdot \ln(x)\text{.}\) Using the numbers weâre given, we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \ln(5^3) = 3\cdot \ln(5)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (b)
    \(\ln(10\cdot 5^3) \)
    Solution.
    When you look at this expression, you see that the input is the product \(10\) and \(5^3\text{,}\) so write down the second law of logarithms \(\ln\Big( x\cdot y \Big)= \ln(x) + \ln(y)\text{.}\) Applying that in our example, we get
    \begin{equation*} \ln(10\cdot 5^3) = \ln(10) + \ln( 5^3 )\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Recopy the first logarithm \(\ln(10)\text{.}\) For the second logarithm, see that the input is a power, so write down the first law of logarithms \(\ln\Big( x^r \Big)= r\cdot \ln(x)\text{.}\) That gives
    \begin{equation*} \ln(10\cdot 5^3) = \ln(10) + 3\cdot \ln( 5 )\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Warning: Note that our three laws of logarithms only apply when the exponential, product, or fraction is inside the logarithm.
    If you try to divide logarithms, such as in \(\dfrac{\ln(5)}{\ln(2)}\text{,}\) you will get something very different than our log laws. This is because of the surprising fact that
    \begin{equation*} \log_b(x) = \dfrac{\ln(x)}{\ln(b)} \end{equation*}
    For example, if you divide \(\dfrac{\ln(5)}{\ln(2)}\text{,}\) you actually get \(\dfrac{\ln(5)}{\ln(2)} = \log_2(5) = 0.9162\dots \text{,}\) which is not the same thing as \(\ln\left(\frac{2}{5} \right)=0.4306\dots\text{.}\)
    Again, for the remainder of the course, we will almost always restrict our attention to natural logarithms. But this last property is useful in many applications, and may help you remember when you can (and when you canât) perform a simplification using the laws of logarithms.

    Section 6.4 Solving Compound Interest for Time

    We can now use the laws of logarithms to help us solve equations where the variable is in the exponent.
    As with everything else in algebra, if we have the equation \(A = B\text{,}\) we can replace it with the equation \(\ln(A) = \ln(B)\text{.}\)

    Exploration 6.6.

    (a)
    Solve for \(x\) satisfying \(2^{3x} = 5\)
    Solution.
    The key idea of algebra is that if you have the equation \(A = B\text{,}\) you can do the same thing to both sides and have another true equation. Here, we are given that
    \begin{equation*} 2^{3x} = 5 \end{equation*}
    That means that we also know that the equation \(\ln(A) = \ln(B)\) is true, replacing \(A=2^{3x}\) and \(B=5\text{.}\) This gives
    \begin{equation*} \ln\Big(2^{3x}\Big) = \ln\Big( 5 \Big) \end{equation*}
    We have the property of logarithms that \(\ln(x^r) = r\cdot\ln(x)\) on the left, to get the equation
    \begin{equation*} 3x \cdot \ln\Big(2\Big) = \ln\Big( 5 \Big) \end{equation*}
    Now, this looks complicated, but we can use a calculator to simplify \(\ln(2) = 0.693\dots\) and \(\ln(5) = 1.609\dots\text{.}\) This gives us the equation
    \begin{equation*} 3x \cdot 0.693{\dots} = 1.609\dots \end{equation*}
    Rearranging the left side, we have
    \begin{equation*} 3\cdot 0.693{\dots}\cdot x = 1.609\dots \end{equation*}
    Using a calculator, we see that \(3\cdot 0.693\dots = 2.079\dots\text{,}\) which gives
    \begin{equation*} 2.079\dots x = 1.609\dots \end{equation*}
    Dividing both sides by \(2.079\dots\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} x = \dfrac{1.609\dots}{2.079\dots} = 0.773\dots \end{equation*}
    You could also have done this with the original \(3x \cdot \ln\Big(2\Big) = \ln\Big( 5 \Big)\) by dividing both sides by \(3\cdot\ln(2)\) to get
    \begin{equation*} x = \dfrac{\ln(5)}{3\cdot \ln(2)} \end{equation*}
    (b)
    Solve for \(x\) satisfying \(4\cdot(1.95)^{2x} = 16\)
    Solution.
    There are two different approaches for solving an equation like this, planning ahead or using brute force.
    Planning Ahead
    We wish to solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} 4\cdot(1.95)^{2x} = 16 \end{equation*}
    We want to eventually introduce logarithms to allow us to apply Log Law 3, to pull the \(2x\) out of the exponent. However, that law will not apply to \(4\cdot (1.95)^{2x}\) because of the coefficient of \(4\text{.}\)
    But we can divide both sides of the equation by 4. This gives us
    \begin{align*} \amp 4\cdot (1.95)^{2x} \amp = \amp 16 \amp \\ \times\frac{1}{4} \amp \amp\amp \amp \times\frac{1}{4} \\ \amp (1.95)^{2x} \amp = \amp 4 \amp \end{align*}
    Now, we can take the logarithm of both sides
    \begin{equation*} \ln\Big( {\color{red}(1.95)}^{\color{blue}2x} \Big) = \ln\Big( 4 \Big) \end{equation*}
    Now we can apply the law of logs \(\ln({\color{red}x}^{\color{blue}r})= {\color{blue}r}\ln({\color{red}x})\) on the left with \(\color{red} x = 1.95\) and \(\color{blue} r = 2x\)
    \begin{equation*} {\color{blue} 2x} \cdot \ln\Big({\color{red} 1.95}\Big) = \ln\Big(4\Big) \end{equation*}
    We can now divide both sides by \(2\cdot \ln(1.95)\) to get
    \begin{equation*} x = \dfrac{\ln\Big(4\Big)}{2\ln\Big(1.95\Big)} \end{equation*}
    This example is exact, but it can be a bit overwhelming. You can also use your calculator to simplify
    \begin{align*} 2x \cdot \ln\Big(1.95\Big) \amp = \ln\Big(4\Big) \\ \\ 2x \cdot 0.667\dots \amp = 1.386\dots \\ \\ x\cdot 1.335\dots \amp = 1.386\dots\\ \\ x \amp = \dfrac{1.386\dots}{1.335\dots} = 1.038\dots \end{align*}
    If you do use your calculator, try to use as many decimal places as possible. Rounding errors can grow if you do a lot of calculations. It is possible to understand how these errors grow, but that question is beyond the scope of this course.
    Brute Force
    We have seen how to solve this equation with a bit of planning ahead. But what happens if you start by taking the natural log of both sides of the quation
    \begin{equation*} 4\cdot(1.95)^{2x} = 16 \end{equation*}
    You get
    \begin{equation*} \ln\Big( {\color{red} 4}\cdot{\color{blue} (1.95)^{2x}} \Big) = \ln\Big(16\Big) \end{equation*}
    On the left side, we have \(\ln\Big( {\color{red} 4}\cdot{\color{blue} (1.95)^{2x}} \Big)\text{.}\) We cannot use Log Law 1, since this is a product. Instead, we need to use Log Law 2 \(\ln({\color{red} a}\cdot {\color{blue} b}) = \ln({\color{red} a}) + \ln({\color{blue} b})\) with \(\color{red} a=4\) and \(\color{blue} b=(1.95)^{2x}\text{.}\) This gives
    \begin{equation*} \ln\Big( {\color{red} 4}\Big) + \ln\Big({\color{blue} (1.95)^{2x}} \Big) = \ln\Big(16\Big) \end{equation*}
    Now we can use Law Log 1 \(\ln(a^r) = r\cdot \ln(a)\) with \(a=1.95\) and \(r=2x\) to get
    \begin{equation*} \ln\Big( { 4}\Big) + 2x\cdot \ln\Big({ (1.95)} \Big) = \ln\Big(16\Big) \end{equation*}
    You can simplify this using a calculator to get
    \begin{equation*} {1.386\dots} + 2\cdot x\cdot 0.667\dots = 2.772\dots \end{equation*}
    You can solve this as usual to get
    \begin{equation*} x = \dfrac{{2.772\dots} - 1.386\dots}{2\cdot 0.667\dots} = 1.038\dots \end{equation*}
    Alternatively, you can solve the exact equation to get
    \begin{equation*} x = \dfrac{\ln(16) - \ln(4)}{2\cdot \ln(1.95)} \end{equation*}
    (c)
    The balance of a certain investment is given by \(F(t) = 10\cdot 2^t\) as a function of the number of years \(t\text{.}\) How long must you wait until the balance reaches 80?
    Solution.
    Note that this specific function is very simple, so it is possible to answer this numerically. After one year, the balance will be \(F(1) = 20\text{,}\) after two years the balance will be \(F(2)=40\text{,}\) and after three years the balance will be \(F(3)=80\text{.}\) It will take 3 years for the balance to reach \(80\text{.}\)
    For more complex equations, we will need to set up and solve an exponential equation. In this case, we want to find a \(t\) such that \(F(t) = 10 \cdot 2^t\) equals 80. This gives us the equation
    \begin{align*} 80 \amp=\amp 10 \cdot 2^t \amp \\ 8 \amp=\amp 2^t \amp \qquad\text{ Multiply both sides by }\dfrac{1}{10}\\ \ln\Big(8\Big) \amp=\amp \ln\Big(2^t\Big) \amp \qquad\text{ Take }\ln\text{ of both sides}\\ \ln\Big(8\Big) \amp=\amp t\cdot \ln\Big(2\Big) \amp \qquad\text{ Log Law 1 } \ln(a^r)=r\ln(a)\\ \dfrac{\ln\Big(8\Big)}{\ln\Big(2\Big)} \amp=\amp t \amp \qquad\text{ Divide both sides by } \ln\Big(2\Big) \end{align*}
    Alternatively, we could have simplified the natural log terms and solved as follows
    \begin{gather*} \ln\Big(8\Big) = t\cdot \ln\Big(2\Big) \\ 2.079{\dots}= t\cdot {0.693\dots} \\ t = \dfrac{2.079{\dots}}{0.693\dots} = 3 \end{gather*}
    We can now apply these techinques to solve the equations that arise in questions of compound interest.

    Exploration 6.7.

    You invest $1000 at 15% interest compounded monthly.
    (a)
    How many years are required until you double your money?
    Solution.
    First, write down the compound interest formula from DefinitionÂ 5.13.
    \begin{equation*} F = P\cdot \left(1 + \frac{r}{m}\right)^{mt} \end{equation*}
    with \(P=1000\text{,}\) \(r=0.15\text{,}\) and \(m=12\text{.}\) Furthermore, we want the future valueto be twice 1000. In other words, we want \(F=2000\) This gives
    \begin{equation*} 2000 = 1000 \cdot \left(1 + \dfrac{0.15}{12}\right)^{12t} \end{equation*}
    Dividing both sides by 1000 gives
    \begin{equation*} 2 = \left(1 + \dfrac{0.15}{12}\right)^{12t} \end{equation*}
    Next, we can simplify this equation using a calculator and the properties of exponentsÂ A.3
    \begin{align*} 2 = \amp \left(1 + \dfrac{0.15}{12}\right)^{12t} \amp \\ 2 = \amp \left(\ 1.0125\ \right)^{12t} \amp \\ 2 = \amp \left(\ 1.0125^{12}\ \right)^{t} \amp \qquad\text{Because } a^{rs}=(a^r)^s\\ 2 = \amp \left(\ 1.16075\dots\ \right)^{t} \amp \end{align*}
    Finally, finish solving the exponential equation as above using the Laws of Logs.
    \begin{align*} \ln\Big(2\Big) = \amp \ln\Big( \left(\ 1.16075\dots\ \right)^{t} \Big) \amp \\ \ln\Big(2\Big) = \amp t\cdot \ln\Big( 1.16075\dots \Big) \amp \qquad\text{Using }\ln(a^r) = r\ln(a)\\ t = \amp \dfrac{ \ln\Big(2\Big)}{\ln\Big( 1.16075\dots \Big) }\amp \qquad\text{Dividing both sides by }\ln\Big( 1.16075\dots \Big)\\ t = \amp 4.64\dots \end{align*}
    With 15% interest, it will take about 4.64 years for the balance to double.
    (b)
    How long will it be until the balance reaches $10,000?
    Solution.
    First, write down the compound interest formula
    \begin{equation*} F = P\cdot \left(1 + \frac{r}{m}\right)^{mt} \end{equation*}
    and plug in the values we are given. Note that we want the time when the future value \(F=10,000\text{.}\) That gives
    \begin{equation*} 10,000 = 1,000 \cdot (1 + \frac{0.15}{12})^{12t} \end{equation*}
    Simplifying this with a calculator and the laws of exponents gives
    \begin{align*} 10 \amp = (1 + \frac{0.15}{12})^{12t}\\ 10 \amp = (1.0125 )^{12t}\\ 10 \amp = (1.16075\dots )^{t} \end{align*}
    Taking \(\ln\) of both sides and applying the laws of logarithms, we get
    \begin{align*} \ln\Big(10\Big) \amp = \ln\Big( (1.16075\dots )^{t} \Big)\\ \ln\Big(10\Big) \amp = t\cdot \ln\Big( 1.16075\dots \Big)\\ \dfrac{\ln\Big(10\Big)}{\ln\Big( 1.16075\dots \Big)} \amp = t \\ t \amp = 14.868\dots \amp \end{align*}
    The balance will increase to $10,000 in about 15 years.

    Exploration 6.8.

    Suppose that a certain asset loses 30% of its value each year. In other words, suppose that the value of the asset after \(t\) years is given by
    \begin{equation*} V(t) = P\cdot (1-0.3)^t \end{equation*}
    (a)
    Suppose that the asset starts with value \(P=1000\text{.}\) Find the value after 12 years.
    Solution.
    The future value function is
    \begin{equation*} V(t) = 1000 \cdot (1-0.3)^t = 1000\cdot 0.7^t \end{equation*}
    After \(12\) years,
    \begin{equation*} V(12) = 1000 \cdot (1-0.3)^{12} = 13.841 \end{equation*}
    After \(12\) years, the value of the asset will be approximately $13.84
    (b)
    Suppose that the value after 12 years is $50. Find the starting value \(P\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The future value function is
    \begin{equation*} V = P \cdot (1-0.3)^t \end{equation*}
    We know that when \(t=12\text{,}\) that \(V=50\text{.}\) Plugging this in gives
    \begin{align*} 50 \amp = P \cdot (1-0.3)^{12}\\ 50 \amp = P \cdot 0.0138\dots\\ \frac{50}{0.138} \amp = P\\ P \amp = 3612.380\dots \end{align*}
    The starting value must have been about $3,612.38
    (c)
    Suppose that the asset starts with a value \(P=1000\text{.}\) Find the number of years \(t\) for the value to decrease to \(100\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The future value function is
    \begin{equation*} V = P \cdot (1-0.3)^t \end{equation*}
    We want the time \(t\) when the value reaches \(V=100\) when from a starting value of \(P=1,000\text{.}\) Plugging this in gives
    \begin{align*} 100 \amp = 1000 \cdot (0.7)^t\\ 0.1 \amp = (0.7)^t\\ \ln\Big(0.1\Big) \amp = \ln\Big((0.7)^t\Big)\\ \ln\Big(0.1\Big) \amp = t\cdot \ln\Big(0.7\Big)\\ t \amp = \dfrac{ \ln\Big(0.1\Big)}{\ln\Big(0.7\Big)} = 6.45\dots \end{align*}
    It will take about 6.45 years for the balance to decline from 1000 to 100.

    Application 6.8. Depreciating Assets and Scrap Value.

    In this class, our focus is on the mathematical tools that underly economics, business, and accounting.
    In accounting, you will learn about how to decide which mathematical too to use (straight line versus declining balance depreciation), and to find a numerical value for the rate of depreciation.
    Rates of depreciation can be found in many ways. For example, you can use the actual sales price for cars based on their mileage to emperically determine how much the value drops per mile.
    But there are also other ways of calculating the rate of depreciation. For example, some assets have a known or conventional "scrap value" and "expected lifespan."
    Once you know the starting value, the scrap value, and the expected lifespan, you can actually compute the yearly depreciation you should use. Mathematically, that means solving
    \begin{equation*} F = P ( 1- r)^t \end{equation*}
    for the rate \(r\) to get
    \begin{equation*} r = 1- \sqrt[t]{\frac{F}{P}} \end{equation*}

    Section 6.5 Applications of Logarithms

    Although logarithms are less familiar than lines, polynomials, and rational functions, they do play an important role in the physical and economic sciences. In this section we will introduce two examples of logarithms. These examples are somewhat simplified for mathematical clarity, but the interested reader is encouraged to continue their study to understand the relevant nuances.
    Logarithmic models often arise when discussing quantities that are more meaningful when compared to a baseline than when viewed as isolated numbers.
    For example, it is hard to say how happy it would make someone to win a $1000 prize. Presumably this win would make someone with $5,000 in their bank account would be much happier than a similar win would make someone with $5,000,000 in wealth.
    Similarly, a cell phone ringer might seem very loud in a quiet library, but the same ringer would sound very quiet at a packed concert.

    Exploration 6.9. Sound Perception and Logarithms.

    Let \(x\) denote the magnitude of a sound, measured by the amplitude of the sound wave itself. The percieved sound level is given by
    \begin{equation*} dB(x) = 10\cdot \log_{10}(x) \end{equation*}
    This formula is a slight oversimplificaiton. See, for example, Wikipedia
    â1â
    en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decibel#Perception
    for more details.
    (a)
    The sound waves of rustling leaves have power \(x=100\text{.}\) Find \(dB(100)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    \(dB(100)=10\cdot\log_{10}(100) = 10\cdot\log_{10}(10^2) = 20\) decibels
    (b)
    The sound waves in a quiet library have power \(10,000\text{.}\) Find \(dB(10,000)\text{.}\) How much louder does this sound compared to rustling leaves?
    Solution.
    \(dB(10,000)=10\cdot\log_{10}(10,000) = 10\cdot\log_{10}(10^4) = 40\) decibels.
    The decibel rating of a quiet library (40 dB) is twice the decibel rating of leaves (20 dB), so the library sounds twice as loud.
    But surprisingly, this apparent doubling is actually the result of a \(\frac{10,000}{100}=100\)-fold increase in the power of the waves.
    (c)
    The sound waves from an alarm clock have power \(100,000,000\text{.}\) Find \(dB(100,000,000)\text{.}\) How much louder does this sound compared to a quiet library?
    Solution.
    \(dB(100,000,000)=10\cdot\log_{10}(100,000,000) = 10\cdot\log_{10}(10^8) = 80\) decibels.
    The decibel rating of an alarm clock (80 dB) is twice the decibel rating of a quiet library (40 dB), so the library sounds twice as loud.
    But surprisingly, this apparent doubling is actually the result of a \(\frac{100,000,000}{10,000}=10,000\)-fold increase in the power of the waves.
    (d)
    How much does doubling the power of the sound waves increase the decibel rating of a given frequency \(x\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    We want to understand the decibel rating of \(2x\text{.}\) Using the definition of \(dB\) gives
    \begin{align*} dB(2x) \amp = 10 \log_{10}(2x) \amp \\ \amp = 10\Big(\log_{10}(2) + \log_{10}(x)\Big) \amp \ln(ab)=\ln(a)+\ln(b)\\ \amp = 10\Big(0.301029 + \log_{10}(x)\Big) \amp \text{calculator}\\ \amp = 3.01029 + 10\log_{10}(x) \amp \\ \amp = 3.01029 + dB(x) \amp \end{align*}
    Doubling the power of the sound waves only increases the decibel rating by about \(3dB\)
    (e)
    How much does increasing the power of the sound waves by a factor of 100 change the decibel rating of a given frequency \(x\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    We want to understand the decibel rating of \(100x\text{.}\) Using the definition of \(dB\) gives
    \begin{align*} dB(10x) \amp = 100 \log_{10}(10x) \amp \\ \amp = 10\Big(\log_{100}(10) + \log_{10}(x)\Big) \amp \ln(ab)=\ln(a)+\ln(b)\\ \amp = 10\Big(2 + \log_{10}(x)\Big) \amp \text{calculator}\\ \amp = 20 + 10\log_{10}(x) \amp \\ \amp = 20 + dB(x) \amp \end{align*}
    Multiplying the power of sound waves by a factor of \(100\) only increases the decibel rating by the addition of \(20dB\).

    Exploration 6.10. Utility and Logarithms.

    Some economists argue that the percieved value of money (often called the utility of money) is logarithmic. Of course, different people will have different utility functions. To see why, we will explore a specific utility function.
    You can find more on this in Chapter 25 of Thinking Fast and Slow. TODO look up and incorporate "Prospect Theory", Feckner, Bernoulli (cited by Thinking Fast and Slow)
    Given life savings of money \(x\) in dollars, let the utility of $\(x\) of these savings be given by
    \begin{equation*} U(x) = 21.7\ln(x) + 150 \end{equation*}
    (a)
    Find the utility of $0, $10, $100, $1000, $10,000, $100,000, and $1,000,000.
    Explain the meaning of these numbers. Why are some negative? Why are some positive? How do they change as your life savings increases?
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|c|c|c|c} \text{dollars }x & 0 & 10 & 100 & 1,000 & 10,000 & 100,000 & 1,000,000 \\ \hline \text{utility }U(x) & DNE & -100.0 & -50.06 & -0.10 & 49.86 & 99.83 & 149.79 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The first thing to note is that \(U(0)\) is undefined. Mathematically, this is because \(\ln(0)\) is undefined. Practically, this is because you would be very unhappy if you did not have any money to use to pay rent, buy food, etc.
    Next, note that this person has negative utility until approximately \(x=1,000\text{.}\) Perhaps this would mean that the individual needs at least $1000 for basic necessities. Then a balance of less than $1000 implies unmet necesseties, which would make the individual unhappy indeed.
    Finally, consider the values above $1000. Note that increasing the balance by a factor of 10 approximately increases their happiness by 50 utility points. Often, this is because the individual will start with a list of very clear and very productive uses of the money. As those clear producive uses are taken care of, the individual needs to look for less clear or less productive uses for their money. Thus, each dollar does not have the same value to the individual!
    Of course, this is an overly simplified example. In fact, utility functions are much more complex. But even these more complex functions will share many of the above properties with our logarithmic utility.
    (b)
    How does doubling a wealth of $\(x\) impact utility?
    Solution.
    We are asked to understand the utility of $\(2x\) dollars. Using the utility function given, we have
    \begin{align*} U(2x) \amp = 21.7\ln(2x) + 150 \amp \\ \amp = 21.7\Big(\ln(2) + \ln(x) \Big) + 150 \amp \ln(ab)=\ln(a)+\ln(b) \\ \amp = 21.7\ln(2) + 21.7\ln(2x) + 150 \amp \\ \amp = 15.04 + 21.7\ln(2x) + 150 \amp \\ \amp = 15.04 + U(x) \amp \end{align*}
    Doubling the amount of money available adds 15.04 utility (or "happiness") points.
    (c)
    How does multiplying a wealth of $\(x\) by a factor of 10 impact utility?
    Solution.
    We are asked to understand the utility of $\(10x\) dollars. Using the utility function given, we have
    \begin{align*} U(10x) \amp = 21.7\ln(10x) + 150 \amp \\ \amp = 21.7\Big(\ln(10) + \ln(x) \Big) + 150 \amp \ln(ab)=\ln(a)+\ln(b) \\ \amp = 21.7\ln(10) + 21.7\ln(x) + 150 \amp \\ \amp = 49.97 + 21.7\ln(x) + 150 \amp \\ \amp = 49.97 + U(x) \amp \end{align*}
    Multipling by 10 the amount of money available adds 49.97 utility (or "happiness") points.
