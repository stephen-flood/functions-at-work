    Chapter 8 Average Rates of Change

    The concepts contained in Chapter 7–9 are all connected in important, but sometimes tricky to follow ways. As you go through this section, you may find it helpful to refer to the diagram below to see how the concepts we have seen so far are related.

    Section 8.1 Average Rates of Change

    Let \(y\) be any function of \(x\text{.}\) That means that any change of input \(\Delta x\) produces a change in the output \(\Delta y\text{.}\) If we average the change in outputs \(\Delta y\) over the inputs \(\Delta x\text{,}\) we get the average change \(\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\text{.}\)
    For reasons we will see in a future section, we will use the terms average change and average rate of change interchangeably.

    Definition 8.1.

    Given a curve \(y=f(x)\) and two input values \(x_1\) and \(x_2\)
    • The change in input that results from moving from \(x_1\) to \(x_2\) is \(\Delta x = x_2-x_1\)
    • The resulting change in output that results from moving from \(x_1\) to \(x_2\) is
      \begin{equation*} \Delta y = y_2-y_1 = f(x_2) - f(x_1) \end{equation*}
      where \(y_1 = f(x_1)\) and \(y_2=f(x_2)\text{.}\)
    • The average rate of change of \(y\) as a result of changing the inputs from \(x_1\) to \(x_2\) is
      \begin{equation*} \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1} = \dfrac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1} \end{equation*}

    Checkpoint 8.2.

    Recall that the total cost function \(C(x)\) is a function of the total number of items \(x\) to be produced. Suppose that increasing your production by 100 items will increase the total cost by $2000. Find the average (aka average rate of) change in the cost.
    Solution.
    We are told that a change of input \(\Delta x = 100\) will result in an increase in total cost of \(\Delta C = 2000\text{.}\) To find the average change, divide that $2000 evenly across the 100 additional items
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{\Delta C}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{2000}{100} = 20 \end{equation*}
    On average, each of the 100 additional items will increase the total cost by $20.
    Average rates are common throughout economics and daily life. When the number of items is large (in the context of macroeconomics), limits show up and calculus begins. But before we get there, we will first explore a number of examples that emphasize the connection between totals, changes and averages/rates.

    Exploration 8.1.

    A total (cumulative) cost function is defined by \(C(x) = 0.1 x^2 + 50 x + 1000 \) in dollars as a function of the total number of items \(x\) to be produced.
    (a)
    Last year, you made 100 items. This year, you are considering increasing your production to 150 items. Find the change in cost and the average rate of change in the cost that would result from this change.
    Solution.
    From the information given in the problem, we can compute the total cost of producing 100 items is
    \begin{equation*} C_1 = C(100) = \$ 7, 000 \end{equation*}
    and that the total cost of producing 150 items would be
    \begin{equation*} C_2 = C(150) = \$ 10, 750 \end{equation*}
    The change in cost between these quantities is
    \begin{equation*} \Delta C = C(150) - C(100) = 10,750 - 7,000 = \$ 3,750 \end{equation*}
    This change is a result of changing the quantity by
    \begin{equation*} \Delta x = 150 - 100 = 50 \text{ items} \end{equation*}
    . Dividing the total cost \(\Delta C\) evenly among the \(\Delta x\) items results in an average cost per item of
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{\Delta C}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{3,750}{50} = 75 \frac{\$}{\text{item}} \end{equation*}
    There were a lot of parts of this calculation. You can either use a the definition \(\dfrac{\Delta C}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{C(x_2) - C(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1} \) which is a single formula that combines all of these steps, or you can organize the different parts separately using the table below. This is called an Average Rate of Change table, often abbreviated AROC.
    \(x_1\) \(x_2\) \(\Delta x\) \(y_1\) \(y_2\) \(\Delta y\) \(\Delta y/\Delta x\)
    \(100\) \(150\) \(50\) \(7000\) \(10750\) \(3750\) \(75\)
    (b)
    Use the cost function above to find the average change in cost between 100 and 125 items, between 100 and 110 items, and between 100 and 101 items.
    Solution.
    \(x_1\) \(x_2\) \(\Delta x\) \(y_1\) \(y_2\) \(\Delta y\) \(\Delta y/\Delta x\)
    \(100\) \(125\) \(\color{red} 125 - 100 = 25\) \(\color{blue} C(100) = 7000\) \(\color{blue} C(125) = 8812.5\) \(\color{blue} C(125) - C(100) \color{green} = 8812.5 - 7000 = 1812.5 \) \(\dfrac{\color{green} 1812.5}{\color{red} 25} = 72.50 \)
    \(100\) \(110\) \(\color{red} 110 - 100 = 10\) \(\color{blue} C(100) = 7000\) \(\color{blue} C(110) = 7710 \) \(\color{blue} C(110) - C(100) \color{green} = 7710 - 7000 = 710 \) \(\dfrac{\color{green} 710}{\color{red} 10} = 71.00 \)
    \(100\) \(101\) \(\color{red} 101 - 100 = 1\) \(\color{blue} C(100) = 7000\) \(\color{blue} C(101) = 7070.1\) \(\color{blue} C(101) - C(100) \color{green} = 7070.1 - 7000 = 70.1\) \(\dfrac{\color{green} 70.1}{\color{red} 1} = 70.10 \)
    (c)
    Suppose that last year you produced 100 items, and that you are planning to increasing production by 5 items this year. Find the average rate of change in cost that results from this change.
    Solution.
    We are given the starting quantity \(x_1 = 5\) and the change in quantity \(\Delta x = 5\text{.}\) Using this, we can reason backward to get the ending quantity \(x_2 = 105\text{.}\) We can use the AROC equation
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{\Delta C}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{C(105) - C(100)}{105-100} = \dfrac{7352.5 - 7000}{5} = 70.5 \end{equation*}
    or create an AROC table
    \(x_1\) \(x_2\) \(\Delta x\) \(y_1\) \(y_2\) \(\Delta y\) \(\Delta y/\Delta x\)
    \(100\) \(105\) \(\color{red} 105 - 100 = 5\) \(\color{blue} C(100) = 7000\) \(\color{blue} C(105) = 7352.5\) \(\color{blue} C(105) - C(100) \color{green} = 7352.5 - 7000 = 352.5\) \(\dfrac{\color{green} 362.5}{\color{red} 5} = 70.50 \)
    The 5 additional items will increase the total cost by an average of 70.50 dollars per item.

    Section 8.2 Graphs of Rates, Changes, and Totals

    Application 8.3. Understanding and Applying "Average Rates of Change".

    We’ve spent a lot of time thinking about “total” and “change” graphs. In each of those examples, we knew the value of the total for each natural number \(1,2,3,\dots\)
    But what happens if we only have information about a few values of \(x\text{.}\) For example, in the graph on the right, we only know the total value on day 0, 5, 10, etc.
    What should we do if we want to know the change between day 0 and 1? We can’t know that number for certain, but we can try to compute the average change.
    Since the value went up 2 units over 5 days, if we divide that change evenly over the 5 days we get the Average Rate Of Change
    \begin{equation*} \text{(AROC)} = \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \dfrac{4-2}{5-0} = \dfrac{2}{5} = 0.4 \end{equation*}
    Summarizing this in a table, we get the row in an Average Rate Of Change (AROC) table:
    \(x_1\) \(x_2\) \(\Delta x\) \(y_1\) \(y_2\) \(\Delta y\) \(\Delta y/\Delta x\)
    \(0\) \(5\) \(\color{red} 5 - 0 = 5\) \(\color{blue} C(0) = 2\) \(\color{blue} C(5) = 4\) \(\color{blue} C(0) - C(5) \color{green} = 2\) \(\dfrac{\color{green} 2}{\color{red} 5} = 0.4\)

    Section 8.3 Velocity and Instantaneous Rates of Change

    So far, we have seen many examples of finding and applying average change. But we have not yet seen why we would call \(\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\) the average rate of change. To understand that side of the story, we need to make a small detour into the physical world.

    Subsection 8.3.1 Measuring Speed with Radar

    Imagine driving down the highway, coming around a corner at 75 miles per hour. Suddenly, you see a speed trap ahead, measuring your speed with radar. How does this work? The detector actually measures your distance at two points in time \(t_1\) and \(t_2\text{.}\)
    Subtracting the distances \(s_1,s_2\) gives your change in distance \(\Delta s\) in miles, and subtracting the times \(t_1,t_@\) gives your change in time \(\Delta t\) in hours (or minutes, or seconds).
    Your estimated velocity will be the average rate of change of your position
    \begin{equation*} \text{average velocity } = \dfrac{\text{change in position}}{\text{change in time}} = \dfrac{s_2-s_1}{t_2-t_1} = \dfrac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} \end{equation*}
    Better radar detectors use smaller \(\Delta t\).
    • \(\Delta t = 1\) hour gives a bad approximation.
    • \(\Delta t = 0.016\) hours (1 minute) gives a better approximation.
    • \(\Delta t = 0.000000000001\) hours gives a very good approximation.
    • By making \(\Delta t\) smaller and smaller, we get closer and closer to your true speed. Mathematically, that means
      \begin{equation*} \text{true speed } = \lim_{\Delta t\rightarrow 0} \dfrac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} \end{equation*}
    To find velocity numerically, compute the average rate (AROC) over smaller and smaller intervals (\(\Delta t\rightarrow 0\)) to see where \(\frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}\) ends up going.
    Remark 8.4. Mathematical notation.
    Average rates of change provide a rough, blocky approximation of change or difference. We highlight this by using the greek capital letter for D, \(\Delta\) to denote the large changes in time and position.
    By letting \(\Delta t\) go to 0, the approximation smooths out, giving the exact or instantaneous rate of change (IROC). We sometimes denote instantanous rates by writing \(\dfrac{ds}{dt}\text{,}\) using the smooth letter \(d\) to emphasize that this is an improved version of the rough AROC.
