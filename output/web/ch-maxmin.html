    Chapter 14 Finding Maxima and Minima

    Section 14.1 Finding Local Extrema

    In any economic system, and in any business, there are many different choices you can make. Of these choices, some are better than others.
    Ideally, you will want to make the best choice. In practice, limits on resources and time often limit the amount of change that is feasible. In these cases, you want to make the locally optimal choice -- the choice that is better than all the alternatives that are "nearby" to your current location.
    Part of the complexity of economic systems are the large number of variables and different ways of comparing which options are "better." In this course, we will focus on functions of a single variable, and we will always compare the magnitude of the outputs.
    To help define our terms precisely, we will start with a more concrete, physical example.

    1. The curve \(y=f\) is horizontal at \(x=-1,1,2\text{.}\)
      This is because \(f'(x)=0\) at these points.
    2. The curve \(y=f\) is has a singularity at \(x=3\text{.}\)
      This is because \(f'(x)=DNE\) at this points.
    3. The curve \(y=f\) is has an endpoint at \(x=-2\) and \(x=4\text{.}\)

    Theorem 14.2.

    A function \(f\) can only have a maxima or minima at \(x=a\) if one of the following are true
    1. \(f\) is horizontal at \(a\text{.}\) This happens when \(f'=0\text{.}\)
    2. \(f\) has a singularity at \(a\text{.}\) This happens when \(f'\ DNE\text{.}\)
    3. \(a\) is an endpoint of the height function \(f\text{.}\)
    The values of \(x\) where \(f'(x)=0\) or \(f'(x)\ DNE\) are called the critical points of \(f\text{.}\) This is because finding these points is one of the most important steps when you are looking for the maxima and minima of the function \(f\text{.}\)
    Warning: Not every critical point is a maxima or minima. In Figure 14.1, \(2\) is a critical point of \(f\) because \(f'(2)=0\text{,}\) and so the function is horizontal at \(x=2\text{.}\) However, \(x=2\) is not a local maxima or minima, since you can increase your height by moving right and decrease it by moving left.
    To be able to check if a point is indeed a maxima or minima, we need a more precise definition.

    Definition 14.3.

    Let \(f\) be a function which is continuous at \(x=a\text{.}\)
    1. \(f\) has a local maxima at \(x=a\) if the graph goes from increasing to decreasing at \(a\) (the function looks like \(\nearrow\,\searrow\) at \(a\)).
    2. \(f\) has a local minima at \(x=a\) if the graph goes from decreasing to increasing at \(a\) (the function looks like \(\searrow\,\nearrow\) at \(a\)).
    3. \(f\) has an extrema at \(x=a\) if there is either a maxima or minima at \(x=a\text{.}\)

    Theorem 14.4.

    Suppose that \(f\) is a differentiable function at \(x\)
    1. height \(f\) is increasing (\(\,\nearrow\,\)) at \(x\) if and only if the slope \(f'(x)\) is positive \((+)\)
    2. height \(f\) is decreasing (\(\,\searrow\,\)) at \(x\) if and only if the slope \(f'(x)\) is positive \((-)\)
    In Exploration 14.1, the function \(f\) is increasing on the interval \((-2,-1) \cup (1,2)\cup (2,3)\) because \(f'\) is positive on that interval.

    Exploration 14.2.

    Let \(f(x) = \dfrac{-x^3}{3} + x + 1\)
    (a)
    Find the critical points of \(f\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Recall that the critical points of \(f\) are the \(x\) values where \(f'(x) =0\) or \(f'(x) \ DNE\text{.}\)
    To find when \(f'=0\) or \(f'\ DNE\text{,}\) we must first find an equation for the derivative \(f'\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} f'(x) = \amp \ddx{ \frac{-1}{3} x^3 + x + 1 } \\ = \amp \ddx{ \frac{-1}{3} x^3 } + \ddx{x} + \ddx{1} \\ = \amp \frac{-1}{3} \ddx{x^3} + 1 + 0\\ = \amp \frac{-1}{3}\cdot 3x^2 + + 1\\ = \amp -x^2 + 1 \end{align*}
    To find when \(f'=0\text{,}\) simply solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} -x^2 + 1 = 0 \end{equation*}
    Solve the equation by factoring. It is easier to factor equations when the highest power term has a factor of 1. To do this, multiply both sides of the equation by -1, simplifying, and factoring
    \begin{align*} (-1) \cdot (-x^2 + 1) \amp =(-1) \cdot = 0 \\ x^2 - 1 \amp = 0 \\ (x+1) (x-1) \amp = 0 \end{align*}
    The only way this can happen is if either the first or second terms equal 0. In other words, solve
    \begin{align*} x+1 = 0 \amp \text{ or } \amp x-1=0 \\ x = -1 \amp \text{ or } \amp x=1 \end{align*}
    We have a singularity when \(f'\ DNE\text{.}\) Looking at the graph of \(f\text{,}\) it does not look like there are any singularities.
    Looking at the function, the derivative \(f'(x) = -x^2+1\) is always defined. This confirms that this function \(f\) does not have any singularities.
    (b)
    Use a calculator to find the local extrema for \(f\)
    Solution.
    Graphing the function we see
    Looking at the graph, we can see that there is a local maxima at \(x=1\) and a local minima at \(x=-1\text{.}\) We also know that there are no other locations where there might be a maxima or minima, even off the screen of the calculator.
    In particular, this tells us that there are no absolute maxima or minima.
    (c)
    Use the first derivative to find the local extrema for \(f\text{.}\) Find the intervals where \(f\) is increasing and when it is decreasing.
    Solution.
    We are asked to locate the maxima and minima without looking at the graph of the function.
    The key idea is that we can use the derivative function \(f'\) to make a very rough sketch of the function by using Theorem 14.4.
    We have already used \(f'\) to find all values of \(x\) where \(f'=0\) or \(f'\ DNE\text{.}\) To make our sketch plot these points on a number line.
    Next, we must compute the derivative \(f'\) to the left and right of each of the points above. There are three intervals: to the left of -1, between -1 and 1, and to the right of 1. You can chose any number in those intervals.
    left of -1
    \(f'(-2) = -(-2)^2 + 1 = -3 = (-)\)
    between -1 and 1
    \(f'(0) = -(0)^2 + 1 = 1 = (+)\)
    right of -1
    \(f'(2) = -(2)^2 + 1 = -3 = (-)\)
    Mark the magnitude of the derivative below the interval, and draw an \(\nearrow\) or \(\searrow\) above the line to indicate the behavior of the function.
    This sign chart is a very rough sketch of the function, but it is complete enough to show that there is a local minima at \(x=-1\) (due to the \(\searrow\,\nearrow\)) and that there is a local maxima at \(x=1\) (due to the \(\nearrow\,\searrow\)).
    It is also clear from the sign chart that the function is increasing on the interval between -1 and 1. In interval notation, it is increasing on the interval \((-1,1)\)
    The function is decreasing on two intervals: to the left of -1 and to the right of 1. In interval notation, \(f\) is decreasing on the interval \((-\infty, -1)\cup (1,\infty)\text{.}\)

    A Strategy for Finding Extrema.

    Let \(f\) be any function which is continuous on an open interval. To find the local extrema,
    1. Use the derivative function \(f'\) to find where \(f'=0\) and when \(f'\ DNE\text{.}\)
      • Polynomials: factor to find the 0’s.
      • Fractions: \(\dfrac{A}{B}\) is equal to zero if when \(A=0\) and \(B\neq 0\text{.}\) \(\dfrac{A}{B}\) is undefined when \(B=0\text{.}\)
    2. Plot these points on a number line. This cuts the real numbers into several intervals.
      Use a closed dot for \(f'=0\) and an open circle for \(f'\ DNE\text{.}\)
    3. Compute \(f'\) on each of the intervals you have found above. Mark whether \(f'\) is \((+)\) or \((-)\) below the interval, and draw \(\nearrow\) or \(\searrow\) above the interval.
    4. Read off the answer to the question, using your sign chart as a very rough sketch of the function.

    Exploration 14.3.

    Let \(f(x) = \dfrac{-x^4}{4} + \frac{8}{3}x^3 + 4\)
    (a)
    Find the intervals where \(f\) is increasing and decreasing..
    Solution.
    First, we must find the critical points. In other words, find the \(x\) such that \(f'(x) =0\) or \(f'(x) \ DNE\text{.}\)
    First, find an equation for the derivative \(f'\text{,}\) and factor it.
    \begin{align*} f'(x) = \amp \ddx{ \frac{-1}{4} x^4 + \frac{8}{3} x^3 + 4 } \\ = \amp \frac{-1}{4}\cdot 4x^3 + \frac{8}{3} \cdot 3x^2 + 0 \\ = \amp -x^3 + 8x^2 \end{align*}
    To find when \(f'=0\text{,}\) simply solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} -x^3 + 8x^2 = 0 \end{equation*}
    Solve the equation by factoring. It is easier to factor equations when the highest power term has a factor of 1. To do this, factor out a \(-x^2\) from both terms
    \begin{align*} (-x^2) \cdot (x - 8) \amp = 0 \end{align*}
    The only way this can happen is if either the first or second terms equal 0. In other words, solve
    \begin{align*} x = 0 \amp \text{ or } \amp x-8=0 \\ x = 0 \amp \text{ or } \amp x=8 \end{align*}
    We have a singularity when \(f'\ DNE\text{.}\) Looking at the graph of \(f\text{,}\) it does not look like there are any singularities.
    Looking at the function, the derivative \(f'(x) = -x^2(x-8)\) is always defined. This confirms that this function \(f\) does not have any singularities.
    To find when the function is increasing and decreasing without looking at the graph of the function, we must use the derivative function \(f'\) to make a very rough sketch of the function by using Theorem 14.4. We will do this by making the sign chart of \(f'\text{.}\)
    Next, we must compute the derivative \(f'\) to the left and right of each of the points above. There are three intervals: to the left of 0, between 0 and 8, and to the right of 8. You can chose any number in those intervals.
    left of 0
    \(f'(-5) = (-5)^3 - 8(-5)^2 = \dots = (-)\)
    between 0 and 8
    \(f'(1) = (1)^3 - 8(-1)^2 = \dots = (-)\)
    right of 8
    \(f'(10) = (10)^3 - 8(10)^2 = \dots = (+)\)
    Mark the magnitude of the derivative below the interval, and draw an \(\nearrow\) or \(\searrow\) above the line to indicate the behavior of the function.
    Using the sign chart, we an read off the behavior of the function. \(f\) is increasing on the interval \((8,\infty)\text{,}\) and \(f\) is decreasing on the interval \((-\infty,0)\cup(0,8)\)
    We can also see that \(f\) has a local maxima at \(x=8\text{,}\) but \(f\) has no local minima (the local minima \(DNE\)).

    We now have a precise, repeatable strategy for finding maxima and minima. The following definition and theorem make this strategy precise.

    Definition 14.5.

    The critical numbers of \(f\) are the numbers \(c\) such that \(\)
    Both
    \(f(c)\) is defined
    And
    either
    \(f'(c) = 0\)
    or
    \(f'(c)\ DNE\)

    Theorem 14.6. The First Derivative Test.

    Suppose that \(c\) is a critical number of a function \(f\text{.}\)
    1. If \(f'\) goes from \((+)\) to \((-)\) at \(c\) (\(f\) goes from \(\nearrow\) to \(\searrow\)) then \(f\) has a local maximum at \(c\text{.}\)
    2. If \(f'\) goes from \((-)\) to \((+)\) at \(c\) (\(f\) goes from \(\searrow\) to \(\nearrow\)) then \(f\) has a local minimum at \(c\text{.}\)
    3. If \(f'\) does not change sign at \(c\) (if \(f\) goes \(\searrow\,\searrow\) or \(\nearrow\,\nearrow\)), then \(f\) does not have a local maximum or minimum at \(c\text{.}\)
    In some cases, we will have more knowledge about the derivative \(f'\) than we have about the height function. The key thing to notice is that we have been using the slope function \(f'\) to teach us about the height function \(f\).

    Exploration 14.4.

    Suppose that \(f(x)\) is an uknown (or mystery) position function, with known derivative function
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) = \dfrac{(x+3)e^x}{(x-2)^2} \end{equation*}
    (a)
    Find the intervals where \(f\) is increasing and decreasing.
    Solution.
    In this example, we are given a factored version of the derivative \(f'\text{,}\) so we do not need to use any of our rules.

    For the first,
    \begin{align*} f' = \amp 0\\ (x+3)e^x \amp =0 \end{align*}
    Because \(e^x \neq 0\) for all \(x\text{,}\) this can only happen if \(x+3=0\text{,}\) which means when \(x=-3\text{.}\)
    For the second,
    \begin{align*} f' \amp DNE \\ (x-2^2) \amp =0 \\ x-2 \amp =0\\ x \amp = 2 \end{align*}
    Plotting these on a number line, we get
    Recall that we were given the derivative function, which we now use to check the sign of each interal. We can either use a calculator as before

    or we can do basic "sign arithmetic" in our head
    \begin{align*} f'(-4) \amp = \dfrac{(-)(+))}{(-)^2} = \frac{(-)}{(+)} = (-)\\ f'(0) \amp = \dfrac{(+)(+)}{(-)^2} = \frac{(+)}{(+)} = (+)\\ f'(3) \amp = \dfrac{(+)(+))}{(+)^2} = (+) \end{align*}

    Find the \(x\) values of all relative extrema.
    Solution.
    Using the sign chart for \(f'\text{,}\) we see that there is a local minima at \(x=-3\text{,}\) and there is no local maxima.

However, these details are beyond the scope of this course, and won’t come up in any of our examples.

    Checkpoint 14.7.

    Find all maxima and minima for the function
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \dfrac{x^2 + 1}{x} \end{equation*}
    Solution.

    Next, we must find all values of \(x\) such that \(f'=0\) and \(f'\ DNE\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} f' \amp =0 \\ (x+1)(x-1) \amp =0\\ x=1,-1 \end{align*}
    similarly
    \begin{align*} f' \amp DNE\\ x \amp =0 \end{align*}
    Plotting these on the number line gives
    Evaluating \(f' = \frac{x^2-1}{x^2}\) on each of these intervals gives

    Note again that the orignal function is undefined (\(DNE\)) at \(x=0\text{,}\) so \(x=0\) cannot be a max or a min regardless of the sign chart.

    1. Find an equation for \(f'\) (if you are not already given it)

    4. Make the sign chart for \(f'\)
    5. Read off the answer.

    Section 14.2 Finding Absolute Extrema

    In this class, we focus on local maxima and minima because of their close connections to calculus and their applications to marginal profit. But in many cases, global (also called absolute) maxima and minima are often of much greater interest. To see why, we begin with one more example.

    Exploration 14.5.

    Suppose that \(P(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x +10 \) is a total profit function.
    (a)
    Find the quantities where all local maximum and minimum profits occur.
    Solution.
    First, find the marginal profit function
    \begin{align*} P'(x) \amp = \ddx{x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x +10 } \\ \amp = \ddx{x^3} - \ddx{ 6x^2 } + \ddx{ 9x } + \ddx{10}\\ \amp = 3x^2 - 6\cdot 2x^1 + 9 + 0\\ \amp = 3x^2 - 12x + 9 \end{align*}
    Next, find when \(P'=0\) or \(P'\ DNE\)
    \begin{align*} P' \amp = 0\\ 3x^2 - 12x + 9 \amp =0\\ 3(x^2 - 4x + 3) \amp =0\\ 3(x-3)(x-1) \amp =0 \end{align*}
    This happens if and only if \(x=3\) or \(x=1\text{,}\) so these are the locations of the horizontal tangents.
    On the other hand, because \(P'\) is a polynomial, it is always defined. Therfore, \(P'\) does not have any sigular points.
    Making a sign chart as usual gives
    According to this analysis, a local maximum of profit occurs at \(x=1\text{,}\) and a local minimum occurs at \(x=3\text{.}\)
    (b)
    If you were able to decide how many items to sell, should you sell 1 item, 3 items, 10 items, or 100 items?
    Solution.
    The easiest way to answer this question is to compute the total profit from each of the number of items given:
    \begin{align*} P(1) \amp = (1)^3 - 6(1)^2 + 9(1) + 10 = 14\\ P(3) \amp = (3)^3 - 6(3)^2 + 9(3) + 10 = 10\\ P(10) \amp = (10)^3 - 6(10)^2 + 9(10) + 10 = 500\\ P(100) \amp = (100)^3 - 6(100)^2 + 9(100) + 10 = 940,910 \end{align*}
    Clearly, if you are given these options you should sell 100 items and make a total profit of $940,910
    (c)
    Use your work above to sketch the total profit function. Use this graph to explain how to decide how many items you should sell.
    Solution.
    Graphing total profit shows that although the function decreases between \(x=1\) and \(x=3\text{,}\) the function rapidly increases after \(x=3\) and then proceeds to grow without bound. In other words, you should always sell as many items as possible.
    The example above helps illustrate the way that some mathematical models oversimplify reality. Mathematically, the function \(P(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 10\) can grow without bound.
    In real life, however, there are always limits on the quanitites involved. In fact, there are only a finite number of atoms in the universe!

    Definition 14.8. Real Life Restricts Quanitites.

    Let \(f\) be anu function and let \(l,r\) be any numbers.
    The restriction of \(f(x)\) to the interval \([l,r]\) is the function that you get if you cut off the parts of the graph of the function that are less than \(l\) and greater than \(r\text{.}\)
    The image below shows the result of restricting \(P(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 10\) to the interval \([0,5]\text{.}\) The restricted function is shown as solid, and the parts of the function that were removed are shown as dotted.

    Theorem 14.9.

    Suppose that \(f\) is a function restricted to the closed interval \([l,r]\text{.}\) This restriction of \(f\) can have an absolute maxima or minima only at
    • one of the endpoints (\(l\) or \(r\))
    • a horiziontal tangent (a point \(c\) where \(f'(c)=0\))
    • a singularity (a point \(c\) where \(f'(c)\ DNE\)).
    That means that to find all of the absolute extrema for \(f\) on a closed interval, it sufficies to find the three kinds of possible locations listed above, and then to compare the height of \(f\) at each of these points.

    Finding Absolute Extrema.

    Suppose you are given a height function \(f\text{.}\) To find the absolute maximum height and minimum height of \(f\) on the interval \([l,r]\text{,}\)
    1. Find the derivative (slope) function \(f'(x)\)
    2. Find all critical points \(c\) where \(f'(c) = 0\) (horizontal tangents) or \(f'(c)\ DNE\) (singluar points).
    3. Find the height of the function at each of the critical points \(c\)
    4. Find the height of the function at the two endpoints (find \(f(l)\) and \(f(r)\)).
    The above computation will give you things like \(P(c_1) = y_1\text{,}\) \(P(c_2) = y_2\text{,}\) \(\dots\text{.}\) If \(y_2\) were the largest of all the \(y\) values, it would be the absolute maximum profit, and it would occur at quanitity \(x_2\) If \(y_3\) were the least of all the \(y\) values, it would be the absolute minimum profit, and it would occur at quanitity \(x_3\text{.}\)

    Section 14.3 Maxima, Minima, and Change Graphs

    Why are the techniques in this chapter important for the study of economics and business?

    Beautiful Fact.

    The total profit function has a local maximum or minimum at the quantity \(c\) if and only if the marginal profit function \(P'\) goes between \((+)\) and \((-)\) at \(c\text{.}\)
    Recall that the derivative \(P'\) and change function \(\Delta P\) are closely related:
    • Total profit is increasing when the change in profit is positive
    • Total profit is decreasing when the change in profit is negative
    This means we can illustrate our method for finding maxima and minima using total and change graphs.
    Recall that the graph on the left gives the total profit from selling a certain number of units, while the graph on the right shows the change in profit due to each unit.
    In the graph above right, the change in profit goes from positive to negative at \(x=6\text{.}\) That means that the total profit has a maximum at \(x=6\) units. This matches our graph of total profit in the graph above left exactly.
    In the graph above right, the change in profit goes from negative to positive at \(x=5\text{.}\) That means that the total profit has a minimum at \(x=5\) units. This also matches exactly our graph of total profit, above left.
